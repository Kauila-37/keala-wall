Land

Three questions to ask before you buy Big Island land

A parcel here can look identical to the one next to it and be worth a fraction of the price — or cost you a hundred thousand dollars more to make livable. The difference is almost never visible from the road. It comes down to three things: what's under the ground, where the water comes from, and what the county will let you build.

Keala M. Wall · Realtor, RB-21841 July 6, 2026 8 min read

Most of the buyers I work with on vacant land are coming from somewhere the questions are simpler. On the mainland, a lot is a lot. Water comes from the street. The rock beneath you is just rock. Here, none of that is safe to assume — and the parcels that look like bargains are usually bargains for a reason.

Here's the order I walk clients through. It's deliberately not the order most people ask in.

1. What is the ground made of — and what is it likely to do?

Hawaiʻi Island is built by active volcanoes, and the U.S. Geological Survey maps that risk directly. The island is divided into lava flow hazard zones 1 through 9, where Zone 1 carries the greatest hazard and Zone 9 the least.1 That single number shapes the price of a parcel, whether you can insure it, and in some cases whether a lender will finance it at all.

This is not an abstraction. The 2018 Kīlauea eruption in lower Puna destroyed 716 homes and covered roughly 14 square miles.1 People who owned in Zone 1 and Zone 2 understood the risk in the abstract. Many had never priced what it meant.

What to do: Get the hazard zone before you get attached to a parcel — it's public information, and I'll pull it for you in a phone call. Then, and only then, call an insurance broker. A property you cannot insure is a property you probably cannot finance.

Low hazard zones command a premium, and they should. But the answer isn't automatically "buy in Zone 9." Plenty of people build thoughtfully in higher zones with eyes open, at prices that make the risk rational. What you cannot do is discover the zone after you're in escrow.

2. Where does the water come from — and where does it go?

Two separate questions, and buyers routinely think about the first while ignoring the second, which is the more expensive one.

Water in

Large stretches of the island have no county water line. Those properties run on rainwater catchment — a roof, gutters, a tank, and a filtration system. It works, hundreds of thousands of people live this way, and it is genuinely pleasant. It also means your water supply is a function of your roof area and the rainfall on your side of the island, which is why the Hilo-versus-Kona question is a plumbing question as much as a lifestyle one. Ask whether a parcel is on county water, on catchment, or on a shared private system, and if it's catchment, ask to see the tank.

Water out

This is the one that surprises people. Hawaiʻi has more cesspools than any other state — roughly 83,000 statewide, of which about 48,600 sit on Hawaiʻi Island.2 Under Act 125, passed in 2017, every one of them must be upgraded or converted by January 1, 2050.2

Conversion typically runs $30,000 to $50,000 per property, and difficult lots run higher.2 If you're buying a parcel with an existing cesspool, or building where a septic system will be required, that is a real number that belongs in your budget today — not in 2049.

A note on numbers you'll see elsewhere: older listings and blog posts still quote cesspool conversion at $15,000–$40,000. That range is out of date. Budget from the current figures above, and confirm with a licensed contractor for your specific lot and soil.

3. What are you actually allowed to build?

A great deal of the affordable acreage on this island is zoned agricultural, and "agricultural" does not mean "unrestricted." Hawaiʻi County's agricultural districts carry minimum lot sizes written into the zoning designation itself: A-1a requires one acre, A-20a requires twenty, and A-200a requires two hundred.3

The number in the zoning code tells you whether the parcel can ever be subdivided, what kind of dwelling is permitted, and what agricultural use the county expects to see. Buyers who plan to split a parcel later, or to put up more than one dwelling, need to confirm this in writing before closing rather than after.

And the cost of running it

Hawaiʻi has the highest residential electricity rates in the United States, around 42¢ per kilowatt-hour as of 2026.4 That fact is why so much of the island's land is developed off-grid, and why solar is less of a lifestyle statement here than a straightforward calculation.

One important correction, because it changes the math and the internet has not caught up: the 30% federal residential solar tax credit (Section 25D) no longer applies to systems installed on or after January 1, 2026. It was eliminated outright — not phased down — by legislation signed in July 2025.5 Commercial and business-use pathways are treated differently. If someone is still selling you a solar package on the strength of a 30% residential credit, ask them to show you the statute.

The through-line

Rock, water, rules. Every genuinely painful land transaction I've seen on this island traces back to one of those three going unasked until it was too late to matter. Every good one started with a buyer who was willing to hear a discouraging answer early, when it was still cheap.

That's the whole job, really. Not talking anyone into a parcel — just making sure that when you fall in love with one, you already know what it is.
