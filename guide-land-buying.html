Buying Land on Hawaiʻi Island

Vacant land here carries layers that don't exist on the mainland — the rock beneath your feet, where the water comes from, and what you can legally build all decide whether a parcel is a wise investment or a costly mistake. This guide walks through what every buyer needs to understand.

1 · Lava hazard zones

The USGS rates every parcel on the island from Zone 1 (highest risk) to Zone 9 (lowest). It's the first thing to check, because zone drives insurance, financing, and resale more than almost any other factor. Lower Puna's Zones 1–2 sit over active rift zones — the 2018 Kīlauea eruption destroyed 716 homes there. Kohala, the island's oldest volcanic area, carries Zone 9 and is the easiest to insure and finance. Look up a parcel's Tax Map Key (TMK) in the county GIS and confirm its zone before you fall in love with it.

2 · Where the water comes from

"Does this property have water?" is a bigger question here than on the mainland. Three systems are common:

  • County water — metered, in established areas like Kona, Hilo, and Waimea. Confirm the meter is active (not just that a line runs nearby) and that no back fees transfer to you.
  • Rainwater catchment — the everyday norm across rural Puna and the Hāmākua Coast. Fully functional when sized and maintained right; inspect the tank (never rusted steel), first-flush diverter, and filtration, and plan for UV or reverse-osmosis treatment for drinking water.
  • Private wells — permitted only in some areas, through the state DLNR Commission on Water Resource Management. Approval isn't guaranteed and can take many months, so never bank on "you can drill a well" without verifying.

3 · What you can build (agricultural zoning)

Most vacant land on the island is zoned agricultural — which doesn't mean you can't build, but it governs how much land you need and what you must demonstrate. Minimum lot sizes run from A-1a (1 acre) and FA (Family Agricultural) districts up through A-20a (20 acres) to A-200a (200 acres). A home on ag land generally has to qualify as a "farm dwelling," and the lower agricultural property-tax rate applies only if you can show active farming — passive ownership can be reassessed at a higher rate. Some parcels also carry a voluntary Important Agricultural Lands (IAL) designation with added benefits in exchange for a long-term farming commitment. Read the zoning before you design a house.

4 · Waste & the 2050 cesspool deadline

Hawaiʻi has more cesspools than any other state — about 83,000 statewide, roughly 48,600 on this island alone. Under Act 125 (2017), every one must be upgraded or converted by January 1, 2050. If you buy a property with a cesspool, you inherit that obligation — budget $30,000–$50,000 for conversion, and expect it sooner near sensitive coastlines. New construction already requires a septic system, and the county requires a licensed engineer's percolation test before approving one.

5 · Off-grid: catchment, solar & self-reliance

Across Puna, Kaʻū, and parts of Hāmākua, thousands of homes run on catchment water and solar power — not as a statement, but because those districts were subdivided without utility infrastructure. With Hawaiʻi's electricity the most expensive in the country (around 42¢/kWh), solar is compelling almost everywhere. One important update: the 30% federal residential solar tax credit ended for systems installed on or after January 1, 2026, so run the numbers on today's costs, not last year's incentives.

6 · Where the farmland is

The island's farm and ranch land concentrates in a few distinct corridors, each with its own climate and crop profile:

  • Kona coffee belt (west slopes) — world-famous coffee country, plus macadamia and diversified ag.
  • Waimea & Kohala ranch country — cool, scenic cattle and horse land anchored by Parker Ranch (founded 1847, among the largest ranches in the U.S.), on the island's lowest-risk Zone 9 in Kohala.
  • Hāmākua Coast — deep, fertile red soil and heavy rainfall; since the sugar industry's close in 1994, it's become diversified farm country at generally more accessible prices.
  • Kaʻū & Volcano — Kaʻū coffee (now internationally recognized), cattle, and cool upland specialty crops.

7 · Due diligence — before you make an offer

Vacant land has fewer built-in protections than buying a home. Confirm:

  • The lava zone — from the TMK/county GIS, never a verbal description.
  • Legal access — a road you can see isn't a road you can legally use; check the title for recorded ingress/egress easements.
  • Water — active county meter, working catchment, valid well permit, or nothing at all?
  • Power — does HELCO service reach the lot, or is it off-grid only (price in solar + batteries)?
  • Septic feasibility — a licensed engineer's perc test before you close.
  • CPR & special districts — Condominium Property Regime lots and State Conservation District land carry their own financing and building limits.
  • On a farm or ranch — verify ag-exemption status, fencing/boundaries, permitted structures, water rights, and ask for a few years of income records on a working operation.
