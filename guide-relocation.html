Relocating to Hawai'i Island

Choosing your side of the island, the real costs, and the mistakes mainlanders make.

The Big Island isn't O'ahu or Maui — it's the largest, most rural, and most varied island in the state, and moving here rewards the people who plan for it. Choosing the right side of the island is the single most important decision you'll make.

1 · Choose your side of the island

There's no single "Big Island experience." Where you land shapes your climate, your commute, your community, and your cost of living — and the island is big enough that a drive from one side to the other takes a couple of hours, not twenty minutes.

  • Kailua-Kona — sunny, social, resort-adjacent, and the most amenities on the island; also the most expensive.
  • Waimea — cool upcountry ranch town with strong schools and a paniolo heritage. Fog and sweaters, not sunburn.
  • Kohala Coast — resort luxury on the dry coast, heritage ranch towns inland, and the island's lowest volcanic risk.
  • Hilo — the county seat: authentic, lush, famously rainy, with a real downtown and the island's most attainable home prices.
  • Puna — fast-growing, jungly, lowest prices, a strong off-grid community, and real volcanic risk. Extraordinary value for the right buyer.
  • Volcano Village — cool, misty rainforest at the edge of the national park; artistic and quiet.

2 · The real cost of living

Hawai'i is expensive, and some costs catch newcomers off guard. Most food is imported, so groceries run well above the mainland. Electricity is the other shock: at about 43¢ per kWh (2026), Hawai'i has the highest residential rates in the country — which is exactly why solar is near-standard here. The trade-off is what disappears: no heating bills, no winter, no grinding commute, and proximity to the ocean and the land that's genuinely hard to price.

3 · What mainland buyers get wrong

After helping people relocate, these are the assumptions that cause the most avoidable — and expensive — mistakes:

  • "I'll buy in Puna and just drive to Kona for work." That's a long, slow commute across the island. Drive your real route at rush hour, in the rain, and at night before you commit to an area.
  • "Lava zones are just a technicality." The 2018 Kīlauea eruption destroyed 716 homes in a matter of weeks. Volcanic risk here is not hypothetical — understand your zone, your insurance, and your financing options first.
  • "The listing says county water, so I'm set." A county line nearby is not the same as an active, metered connection. Verify it with the Department of Water Supply.
  • "I'll rent first, then decide." This is actually the right instinct — but rent in the area you're seriously considering, and experience it through a full season before buying.

4 · A practical move checklist

  • Visit more than once, in different seasons — trade winds, vog, and tourism traffic all change the picture.
  • Drive your intended daily commute at actual commute hours, not midday.
  • Verify internet and cell coverage at the specific address; both vary widely in rural areas.
  • Plan for a car — public transit is limited and distances are large.
  • Line up quotes early for shipping a household or a vehicle, and expect several weeks of transit.
  • Handle Hawai'i vehicle registration, a safety inspection, and your driver's license soon after you establish residency.
  • Find a primary-care physician early — specialist wait lists can be long.
  • Plug into the community: neighborhood associations, farmers markets, and local events are how the Big Island actually works.
