The Four Things That Matter Most

What every land buyer needs to understand.

The handful of factors that actually change whether you should make an offer. Every figure below is sourced; see the Sources page.

01

Lava hazard zones

The USGS rates every parcel on the island from Zone 1 (highest risk) to Zone 9 (lowest). It's the first thing to check, because zone drives insurance, financing, and resale more than almost anything else. Lower Puna's Zones 1–2 sit over active rift zones — the 2018 Kīlauea eruption destroyed 716 homes there. Kohala, the island's oldest volcanic area, carries Zone 9 and is the easiest to insure and finance. Always confirm a parcel's zone before you fall in love with it.

Source: USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory
02

Where the water comes from

"Does this property have water?" is a bigger question here than on the mainland. Three systems are common: county water (metered, in established areas like Kona, Hilo, and Waimea), rainwater catchment (the everyday norm across rural Puna and the Hāmākua Coast — fully functional when sized and maintained right), and private wells (permitted only in some areas, through the state DLNR). Which one a parcel uses affects both the cost to develop it and your daily life. Verify the actual, active source — not the seller's description.

Source: Hawaiʻi County Dept. of Water Supply · DLNR CWRM
03

What you can build (agricultural zoning)

Most vacant land on the island is zoned agricultural — which doesn't mean you can't build, but it governs how much land you need and what you must demonstrate. Minimum lot sizes run from A-1a (1 acre) to A-20a (20 acres) up to A-200a (200 acres). A home on ag land generally has to qualify as a "farm dwelling," and the lower ag property-tax rate applies only if you can show active agricultural use. Read the zoning before you design a house.

Source: Hawaiʻi County Planning — Zoning & Land Use (Chapter 25)
04

Waste & the 2050 cesspool deadline

Hawaiʻi has more cesspools than any other state — about 83,000 statewide, roughly 48,600 on this island alone. Under Act 125 (2017), every one must be upgraded or converted by January 1, 2050. If you buy a property with a cesspool, you inherit that obligation — budget $30,000–$50,000 for conversion, more near sensitive coastlines where the county may require it sooner. New builds already require a septic system and an engineer's percolation test.

Source: U.S. EPA — Cesspools in Hawaii · Hawaiʻi Dept. of Health
Off-Grid

Solar, Catchment & Self-Reliance

Living off the grid is normal here.

Across Puna, Kaʻū, and parts of Hāmākua, thousands of homes run on catchment water and solar power — not as a statement, but because those districts were subdivided without utility infrastructure. With Hawaiʻi's electricity the most expensive in the country (~42¢/kWh), solar is compelling almost everywhere.

Worth knowing: the 30% federal residential solar tax credit ended for systems installed on or after January 1, 2026. Factor solar into a purchase on today's real numbers, not last year's incentives.
Sources: U.S. EIA · EnergySage

Before You Make an Offer

A short due-diligence checklist

Vacant land has fewer built-in protections than buying a home. Before you commit, confirm:

  • The lava zone — look up the parcel's Tax Map Key (TMK) in the county GIS; never rely on a verbal description.
  • Legal access — a road you can see isn't a road you can legally use. Check title for recorded ingress/egress easements.
  • Water — active county meter, a working catchment system, a valid well permit, or nothing at all?
  • Power — does HELCO service reach the lot, or is it off-grid only (price in solar + batteries)?
  • Septic feasibility — the county requires a licensed engineer's perc test before approving a system.
  • CPR & special districts — Condominium Property Regime lots and Conservation-District land carry their own financing and building limits.
