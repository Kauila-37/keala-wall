Life · Living on Hawaiʻi Island

Before the property
comes the place.

The Big Island isn't Oʻahu or Maui — it's the largest, most rural, and most varied island in the state. Choosing the right side of it is the most important decision you'll make.

Find Your Side

One island, a dozen different lives.

There's no single "Big Island experience." Where you land shapes your climate, your commute, your community, and your cost of living. Here's the honest shorthand for the areas people actually move to.

Kailua-Kona

Sunny, social, resort-adjacent, and the most amenities on the island — also the most expensive. The familiar "Hawaiʻi" most transplants picture.

Waimea

Cool upcountry ranch town in the shadow of Parker Ranch — strong schools, real community, and a paniolo heritage. Fog and sweaters, not sunburn.

Kohala Coast

Resort luxury on the dry coast; heritage ranch towns inland. The island's oldest land, and its lowest volcanic risk.

Hilo

The county seat — authentic, lush, and famously rainy, with a real downtown and the island's most attainable home prices. Not a tourist town.

Puna

Fast-growing, jungly, and the island's lowest prices — with a strong off-grid community and real volcanic risk. Extraordinary value for the right buyer.

Volcano Village

Cool, misty rainforest at the edge of the national park — an artistic, quiet community unlike anywhere else in Hawaiʻi.

The Reality

Cost of Living

Honest about the trade-offs.

Hawaiʻi is expensive, and some costs catch newcomers off guard — most food is imported, so groceries run well above the mainland. Electricity is the other shock: at roughly 42¢ per kWh, Hawaiʻi has the highest rates in the country, which is exactly why solar is near-standard here.

The trade-off is what disappears: no heating bills, no winter, no grinding commute — and proximity to the ocean, the land, and a genuine community that's hard to price.

Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration

Making the Move

Relocation, done right

After helping people move here, the pattern is clear: the ones who choose well slow down first. A few essentials:

  • Visit more than once, in different seasons — trade winds, vog, and tourism traffic all shift the picture.
  • Drive your real commute at actual commute hours, in the rain and at night — island geography shrinks your practical options fast.
  • Verify internet and cell coverage at the specific address; both vary widely in rural areas.
  • Rent before you buy if you can — six to twelve months on-island leads to far better location decisions.
  • Plan for a car, and handle Hawaiʻi vehicle registration and licensing soon after you establish residency.
